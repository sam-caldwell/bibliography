Summary

A landmark Supreme Court case that addressed whether a public figure could recover damages for emotional distress caused by a satirical magazine parody. The Court held that the First Amendment protects even offensive, outrageous satire about public figures, provided there is no false statement knowingly or recklessly made. This decision underscored the importance of robust free speech protections in public discourse, even when that speech is crude or insulting.
